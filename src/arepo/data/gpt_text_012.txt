COOKING, AS A RECIPE THAT REFUSES TO BE MEASURED

Begin with hunger, but not just the stomach kind. Begin with the desire to make the day a little more inhabitable.

Find an onion. Peel away its papery defenses and notice how every layer is a smaller version of the last. Chop it and accept the tears as a fee paid to the gods of flavor. Put it in a pan with heat and fat and listen for the first soft sizzle, the sound of raw turning into sweet.

Add garlic when the onion is halfway to golden, because garlic is impatient and will burn if you treat it like an onion. Smell the air change. This is alchemy with groceries.

Now choose a direction. Do you want comfort? Add tomatoes, let them collapse into a sauce that tastes like summer memories and patience. Do you want brightness? Add citrus at the end, because brightness is fragile. Do you want depth? Add mushrooms or roasted things, ingredients that have already been through a little hardship and learned to taste like earth.

Salt is not just seasoning; it is communication. It tells flavors to speak up. Add it carefully, then taste, then adjust. Taste again. Cooking is a conversation with reality. Reality answers honestly.

If you add herbs, remember they are two species: those that like heat and those that don’t.
Some want to simmer and give up their secrets slowly. Others want to arrive late, like a friend who shows up at the end of the party and makes everyone laugh.

When it’s done, serve it in a way that feels like respect.
Eat slowly enough to notice what you made.
The point is not perfection. The point is that you took raw materials and turned them into something that can nourish a body and, briefly, a life.

And if it’s not great, congratulations: you have learned something.
That’s the real ingredient that keeps cooking interesting.
